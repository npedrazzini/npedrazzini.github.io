  • Questions


    1. Started in the 18th century in Great Britain, the industrial mechanization saw a dramatic acceleration in the 19th century. Given large enough datasets, is it possible to automatically detect changes in the meaning of words associated with this process? And...
    2. ... Would the changes detected reflect what the traditional scholarship on the subject (e.g. ???) say on the matter?

  • Challenge


    Historical data is messy, full of orthographic variation, and, most often than not, comes from OCR'd material (i.e. it is full of OCR errors). This could make language models trained on it not very high-quality.

  • Data


    Digitized 19th-century newspaper collection comprising a total of ca. 4.5 billion tokens, spanning the period 1800-1920.

  • (Pre-)processing


    The corpus was divided into bins of 10 years each, to obtain models each representing one decade in the 19th century. Each bin was preprocessed by lowercasing the text, removing punctuation and any word with less than 2 characters.

  • Evaluation


    The results from change point detecetion were compared to the semantic changes indicated by the traditional literature, showing that, in fact, our diachronic models largely reflected what claimed there. The clustering with DTW is still experimental and there is currently no well-established validation method for it. Change point detection on its own, however, suggests that, given their good-quality and the positive confirmation from the scholarship, diachronic models as the ones described can be used to explore new research questions not yet asked by the traditional literature.
